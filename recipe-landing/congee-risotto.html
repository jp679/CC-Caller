Congee Risotto

Two of the world's greatest comfort foods, merged. Arborio rice cooked low and slow with ginger-infused stock until it breaks down into a silky, porridge-like richness — finished with crispy shallots, a jammy egg, and a drizzle of chili oil.

Prep
20 min
Cook
50 min
Serves
4
Difficulty
Medium
🍲
the recipe

Ingredients & Method

The trick is patience and extra stock. You're cooking past the al dente stage on purpose — letting the rice break down until it's creamy, starchy, and impossibly comforting.

Ingredients

Serves 4

The Base

  • Arborio rice 1½ cups
  • Chicken or vegetable stock 8 cups
  • Fresh ginger, sliced into coins 3-inch piece
  • Garlic cloves, smashed 4
  • Neutral oil (grapeseed or peanut) 2 tbsp
  • Dry white wine ½ cup
  • White miso paste 1 tbsp
  • Fish sauce 1 tsp
  • Sesame oil 1 tsp

The Finish

  • Eggs 4
  • Shallots, thinly sliced 3 large
  • Scallions, thinly sliced 4
  • Crispy chili oil (Lao Gan Ma or similar) to taste
  • White pepper generous
  • Toasted sesame seeds 1 tbsp
  • Fresh cilantro handful

Method

1

Infuse the Stock

Bring the stock to a simmer in a saucepan with the ginger coins and smashed garlic. Let it steep on low heat for at least 15 minutes while you prep everything else. Fish out the ginger and garlic before using, or leave them in if you want a more aggressive ginger punch.

Chef's tip: This is where all the depth comes from. Stale, cold stock out of the box won't cut it — warming and infusing it first is the difference between flat and alive.
2

Fry the Shallots

While the stock infuses, heat a generous glug of neutral oil in a small saucepan over medium heat. Add the sliced shallots and fry, stirring often, until deeply golden and crisp — about 8 minutes. Drain on paper towels and season with a pinch of salt immediately. Reserve the shallot oil.

Chef's tip: Watch these like a hawk. They go from golden to burnt in about 20 seconds. Pull them slightly before you think they're done — they'll darken as they drain.
3

Toast and Deglaze

Heat the shallot oil (plus a splash of neutral oil if needed) in a heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the rice and stir constantly for 2 minutes until the grains are translucent at the edges and smell nutty. Pour in the white wine and stir until it's fully absorbed.

4

The Long Simmer

Here's where congee and risotto meet: add the warm infused stock one ladleful at a time, stirring frequently. But unlike a classic risotto where you stop at al dente, keep going. Add stock, stir, let it absorb, repeat. After about 35–40 minutes the rice should be broken down, creamy, and porridge-like — looser than risotto, thicker than congee. You're aiming for something that flows slowly off the spoon.

Chef's tip: If you run out of stock and the consistency isn't loose enough, switch to hot water. The starch is already released — you just need liquid now.
5

Season the Base

Kill the heat. Stir in the white miso paste, fish sauce, and sesame oil. Taste it — it should be deeply savory with a gentle ginger warmth in the background. Finish with a generous crack of white pepper. Adjust salt if needed, but go easy — the toppings bring their own salinity.

6

Make the Jammy Eggs

Bring a small pot of water to a rolling boil. Gently lower the eggs in and cook for exactly 6 minutes and 30 seconds. Transfer to an ice bath immediately. Once cool, peel and halve. The yolk should be bright orange and just barely set — custardy, not runny.

Chef's tip: Start timing the moment the eggs hit the water, not when it returns to a boil. The 6:30 mark is non-negotiable for that perfect jammy center.
7

Bowl and Serve

Ladle the congee risotto into warm bowls. Nestle two egg halves into each portion. Crown with crispy shallots, sliced scallions, a scattering of sesame seeds, and torn cilantro. Finish with a generous drizzle of chili oil. Eat it while it's hot — it thickens as it sits, and you want that first spoonful to be liquid velvet.
